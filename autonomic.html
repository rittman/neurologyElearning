Autonomic symptoms

Abnormalities of the autonomic nervous system appear many years before the onset of Parkinson's disease.

The main autonomic symptoms to keep in mind are below. Click for more details on each symptom.

Early satiety

Slowed gastric emptying leads to a feeling of fullness early on whilst eating meals.

Constipation

Almost universal in Parkinson's disase and usually starts many years before a diagnosis is made.

Changes in sweating

People with Parkinson's disease often report their skin feels different, sometimes oily or dry.

Sexual symptoms

Erectile dysfunction commonly occurs a few years prior to the onset of motor function in men. Vaginal dryness can affect women.

Postural hypotension

A feeling of light-headedness on standing, sometimes accompanied by falls or faints. It is unusual to occur early in Parkinson's disease; significant early postural hypotension should prompt you to consider Multiple System Atrophy.

The definition of postural hypotension is a reduction in systolic blood presure within 3 minutes of standing. This is often associated with no compensatory change in the pulse rate.

Nocturia

Although many older people will experience nocturia, people with Parkinson's disease pass on average 40% of their daily urine output overnight, with some passing up to 75%.

Managing autonomic symptoms

Non-pharmacological treatments of autonomic symptoms

The mainstay of managing autonomic dysfunction is non-pharmacological. Below is a table outlining the main non-pharmacological treatments for each of the symptoms listed above. The point of this table is to highlight the wide range of non-pharmacological (often common sense) ways to approach distressing symptoms.

Pharmacological treatments with their rationale and main side-effects are outlined in the table below. These are symptomatic treatments, and often patients will have to stop these medications because of side-effects or absence of benefit.

Postural hypotension in particular may be exacerbated by dopaminergic therapy, in some cases severe enough to render the patient bed-bound and unable to walk. In this situation reducing the levodopa dose may be the most effective treatment for debilitating postural hypotension.
