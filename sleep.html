Sleep disorders

A surprisingly large number of sleep disorders are associated with Parkinson's disease. Recognising and addressing them can have a large impact on quality of life.

REM-sleep Behavioural Disorder

REM-sleep Behavioural Disorder (RBD) is associated with synuclein pathology (Parkinson's disease and Multiple System Atrophy) and usually precede motor symptoms by years or decades. The symptoms are usually reported by the bed partner who describes patients acting out their dreams such as shouting, kicking or punching. The mechanism is failure of the usual "off switch" of motor function in the brainstem nuclei of thalamocortical pathways during REM sleep so that movements are not suppressed during dreams. Linked to this, many people with Parkinson's disease have vivid dreams.

The presence of RBD in otherwise asymptomatic people is a strong predictor of later Parkinson's disease. Recent studies suggest that up to 80% of people with RBD may develop Parkinson's disease after 16 years.

Treatment with small doses of Clonazepam (250-500 microgrammes) is usually effective if the symptoms are troublesome.

Other sleep abnormalities

Abnormal sleep architecture

Parkinson's disease increases the latency to sleep onset and reduces the time spent in stage 3 and 4 (deep) sleep. This combination leads to the symptoms of both primary and secondary insomnia.

Although there may be multiple factors at play, degeneration of brainstem nuclei in the thalamocortical pathways and disruption of circardian rhythm are major factors.

Sleep attacks

These are particular associated with dopamine agonist medications. Sleep attacks are characterised by sudden attacks of sleepiness, in its most severe form sleep attacks can be similar to narcolepsy.

Restless legs and periodic limb movements

Restless legs syndrome is an overwhelming sensation to move the legs, it may be accompanied by tingling, burning, throbbing or itching usually at night. It is treatable with dopamine agonists.

Periodic limb movements are a related symptom of repetitive movements that can disrupt sleep.

Central apnoea

Sleep apnoea is symptomatically reported by bed partners as episodic cessation of breathing, often accompanied by heavy snoring. Central sleep apnoea occurs in Parkinson's disease, but early central sleep apnoea is a feature of Multiple System Atrophy.
